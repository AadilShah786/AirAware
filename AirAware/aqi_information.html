    The Air Quality Index, or AQI, is a standardised system used worldwide to assess and communicate the quality of the air we breathe. It provides a numerical value and color-coded scale to inform the public about air pollution levels and their potential health impacts. The AQI serves as a vital tool in promoting air quality awareness and public health.

    The AQI is calculated based on the concentrations of specific air pollutants known to have adverse health effects. These primary pollutants include:

    • Particulate Matter (PM2.5 and PM10): Tiny particles or droplets in the air that can be inhaled into the lungs. PM2.5 refers to particles with a diameter of 2.5 micrometers or smaller, while PM10 includes larger particles up to 10 micrometers in diameter.
      Ground-Level Ozone (O3): A secondary pollutant formed by chemical reactions between volatile organic compounds (VOCs) and nitrogen oxides (NOx) in the presence of sunlight.
      Carbon Monoxide (CO): A colorless, odorless gas produced by the incomplete combustion of fossil fuels.
      Sulfur Dioxide (SO2): A gas primarily emitted from the burning of sulfur-containing fuels, such as coal and oil.
      Nitrogen Dioxide (NO2): A reddish-brown gas produced by combustion processes, particularly in vehicles and industrial facilities.

    • The AQI provides a simple and easy-to-understand way to convey the level of air pollution to the public. It is calculated using a sub-index for each of the primary pollutants mentioned above, and then the maximum sub-index value is selected to represent the overall AQI value for a given location. The process involves the following steps:

    • 1. Calculate Sub-Indices: The concentration of each primary pollutant is transformed into a sub-index, indicating the level of air quality associated with that pollutant. These sub-indices are calculated using specific equations.
      2. Select the Maximum Sub-Index: The highest sub-index value among all the primary pollutants is chosen as the overall AQI value for that location.
      3. Color-Coding: The AQI value is associated with a specific color code, ranging from "Good" (green) to "Hazardous" (maroon), to make it easier for the public to interpret.

    • The AQI is divided into different categories, each corresponding to a specific range of AQI values. These categories provide clear information about the air quality and its potential health impacts. The categories typically include:

    • - Good (0-50): Air quality is satisfactory, posing little or no risk to health.
      - Moderate (51-100): Air quality is acceptable; however, there may be a concern for some people who are unusually sensitive to air pollution.
      - Unhealthy for Sensitive Groups (101-150): Members of sensitive groups, such as children, the elderly, and individuals with respiratory conditions, may experience health effects. The general public is not likely to be affected.
      - Unhealthy (151-200): Everyone may begin to experience health effects, and members of sensitive groups may experience more serious health effects.
      - Very Unhealthy (201-300): Health alert: everyone may experience more serious health effects.
      - Hazardous (301-500): Health warning of emergency conditions: the entire population is more likely to be affected

    • The AQI is not just a number; it serves a crucial role in safeguarding public health and the environment. Here's why AQI matters:

    • Health Impacts: Poor air quality can lead to a range of health problems, including respiratory issues, cardiovascular disease, and even premature death. Understanding the AQI can help individuals take protective measures.
      Vulnerable Groups: Certain groups, such as children, the elderly, and individuals with pre-existing health conditions, are more susceptible to the adverse effects of poor air quality. The AQI helps them make informed decisions to reduce exposure.
      Environmental Impact: Air pollution contributes to environmental degradation, including smog formation, acid rain, and damage to ecosystems. Monitoring air quality through the AQI is essential for environmental protection.
      Regulatory Compliance: The AQI is used by regulatory agencies to set air quality standards and assess compliance. It guides policy and regulatory decisions aimed at reducing air pollution.
      Public Awareness: The AQI raises public awareness about air quality and encourages individuals and communities to take actions to reduce pollution and improve air quality.

    • The AQI for a monitoring location is calculated using sub-indices for individual pollutants. These sub-indices are determined based on 24-hourly average concentration values (8-hourly in the case of CO and O3) and specific health breakpoint concentration ranges for each pollutant.
      To calculate an overall AQI, data must be available for a minimum of three pollutants, out of which one must be either PM2.5 or PM10. Additionally, a minimum of 16 hours' data is necessary to calculate sub-indices.
      Even if there's insufficient data to determine the overall AQI, sub-indices for monitored pollutants are still calculated and disseminated. These individual pollutant-wise sub-indices provide air quality status for each specific pollutant.
      Real-Time Web-Based System: The AQI is provided in real-time through an automated web-based system. It captures data from continuous monitoring stations without human intervention and calculates the AQI based on running average values.
      For example, the AQI at 6 am on a given day incorporates data from 6 am on the previous day up to the current day.
      Manual Monitoring Stations: For manual monitoring stations, an AQI calculator is developed, allowing data to be entered manually to obtain the AQI value.
